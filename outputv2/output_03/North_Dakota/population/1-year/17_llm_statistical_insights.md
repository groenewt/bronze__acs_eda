1. MEAN vs MEDIAN:
   The mean (average) is a measure of central tendency that takes into account all values in the dataset, while the median represents the middle value when the data is ordered from least to greatest. In some cases, such as income or poverty status, the mean might be skewed higher due to extreme values (outliers), whereas the median would remain unaffected by these outliers and thus provide a more robust measure of central tendency. For instance, if 5 people earn $10 million each while all others earn $20,000, the mean income would be significantly higher than the median income, suggesting that the distribution is skewed to the right (positively skewed).

2. SKEWNESS:
   Skewness measures asymmetry of a data set around its mean. A positive skew indicates a longer tail extending towards more extreme values on the right side, while negative skew shows a tail extending towards lower values. The higher the absolute value of skewness (e.g., 2 or greater for some variables), the more pronounced the skew is in the data distribution. For example, if incomes follow a positively skewed pattern with many individuals earning significantly less than average, it would have a high positive skew indicating that most people fall below the median income but there are outliers at the higher end.

3. KURTOSIS:
   Kurtosis measures the "tailedness" of a distribution; values above 3 indicate peaked or heavy-tailed distributions (leptokurtic), while values between 3 and infinity suggest platykurtic distributions with lesser tails, and exactly 3 indicates normality. High kurtosis implies that the data has more than expected extreme values compared to a normal distribution. For instance, if many people in the income variable earn significantly above or below average, it would show high kurtosis indicating heavy tails and potential outliers.

4. VARIANCE/STD DEV:
   Variance is a measure of dispersion that tells us how far data points are from the mean, while standard deviation (SD) is the square root of variance, providing a more intuitive scale for understanding spread. Higher variability or SD in variables like income per hour, total annual hours worked, or poverty status suggests greater volatility and disparity among observations compared to other variables such as age or interest/dividend income.
